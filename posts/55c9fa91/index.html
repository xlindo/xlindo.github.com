Equations

95 - T5

  • In practice, the slope of the length is often determined as an averaged value using all the available data rather than as a pointwise estimate, that is,
    • $\hat{L}_{t}^{\prime}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}|\Gamma|_t}{N}$,
    • where $\hat{L}_{t}^{\prime}$ is the averaged slope of the length and $N$ is the number of data points used.

96 - T7

  • Similarly, the averaged entropy can be calculated by taking the sum of the individual instantaneous values, that is,
    • $\overline{H}_{t}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} H_{i}$

97 - T3

  • For the remainder of this section, it will be assumed that the stochastic process is driven by normally distributed white noise and can be written as
    • $y_{t}=G\left(z^{-1}\right) e_{t}$
    • where $G$ is some arbitrary transfer function.

Segmentation Index

  • A preliminary segmentation index can be written as
    • $\eta_{S E G}=\left|\Delta \hat{H}-\Delta H_{t}\right|$
    • where $\Delta \hat{H}$ is the differential entropy of the current model and $\Delta H_t$ is the differential entropy of the current point.
  • Let the residual differential entropy, $\Delta H_{d a t a}(t)$, be defined as
    • $\Delta H_{r e s}=\left|\Delta \hat{H}-\Delta H_{d a t a}(t)\right|$
    • where $\Delta H_{d a t a}(t)$ is the differential entropy of the original data used to fit or validate the model.
  • Irrespective of the source of data, the threshold for the detection of change can be written as
    • $\varepsilon_{S E G}=K+\Delta H_{r e s}$
    • where $K$ is the sensitivity factor.

Theorems

  • Theorem 1: Consider a stochastic process, $\hat{y}_{t}$, driven by a zero-mean white noise sequence with standard deviation $\sigma_{\mathcal{w}}$. Assume that the standard deviation of $\hat{y}_{t}$ is given as $\sigma_{\mathcal{y}}$ and the autocovariance at lag $k$ is given as $\gamma(k)$. Furthermore, assume that the process of interest is given as
    • $\overline{y}_{t}=\left|\hat{y}_{t}\right|$,
    • which implies that $\overline{y}_{t}$ will follow a folded normal distribution. Then, the autocovariance of $\overline{y}_{t}$ can be determined as follows,
      • $\gamma_{Y}(k)=\frac{2 \sqrt{\sigma_{y}^{4}-\gamma(k)^{2}}}{\pi}-\frac{\gamma(k)}{\pi}\left(\pi-2 \arctan \left(-\frac{\sqrt{\sigma_{y}^{4}-\gamma(k)^{2}}}{\gamma(k)}\right)\right)-\mu_{\bar{y}}^{2}$
      • where the arctangent function is taken based on the sign of $\gamma(k)$.
  • Corollary 1: pointwise
    • For a process $\hat{y}_{t}$ given as
      • $\hat{y}_{t}=\frac{\beta z^{-1}-\beta z^{-2}}{1+\alpha z^{-1}} e_{t}$
      • with $e_{t} \sim N\left(0, \sigma_{w}^{2}\right)$ and assuming stationarity of the process, then the individual $\hat{y}_{t}\sim N\left(0, \sigma_{y}^{2}\right)$,
    • $\operatorname{cov}\left(\left|\hat{y}_{t}\right|,\left|\hat{y}_{t-k}\right|\right)=\frac{\beta^{2} \sigma_{w}^{2}}{(1-\alpha) \pi}\left(\begin{array}{l}{2 \sqrt{4-(-\alpha)^{2 k-2}(\alpha+1)^{2}}-4} \ {+(-\alpha)^{k-1}(\alpha+1)(\pi-2 \arctan (\xi))}\end{array}\right)$
      • where
      • $\xi=\frac{\sqrt{4-(-\alpha)^{2 k-2}(\alpha+1)^{2}}}{(-\alpha)^{k-1}(\alpha+1)}$
  • Theorem 2: The expected pointwise slope of the length for a process described by Equation (97) is given as
    • $\begin{array}{l}{E\left(L_{t}^{\prime}\right)=E\left(\left|\left(1-z^{-1}\right) G\left(z^{-1}\right) e_{t}\right|\right)=\mu_{|\hat{y}|}} \ {\operatorname{var}\left(L_{t}^{\prime}\right)=\sigma_{|\vec{y}|}^{2}}\end{array}$
  • Theorem 3: The expected pointwise entropy for a process described by Equation (97) is given as
    • $\begin{aligned} E\left(H_{t}\right) &=E\left(\log \left(L_{t}^{\prime}\right)\right)=E\left(\log \left(\left|\hat{y}_{t}\right|\right)\right) \ &=-\frac{1}{2} \log 2+\log \sigma_{\hat{y}}-\frac{1}{2} \gamma \end{aligned}$
    • where $\sigma_{\hat{y}}$ is the variance of $\hat{y}_{t}=\left(1-z^{-1}\right) G\left(z^{-1}\right) e_{t}$ and $\gamma$ is the Euler-Mascheronit constant.
  • Theorem 4: The variance of the pointwise entropy is
    • $\operatorname{var}\left(H_{t}\right)=\frac{\pi^{2}}{8}$
  • Theorem 5: The expected value of the averaged slope $\hat{L}_{t}^{\prime}$ is the same as that of the instantaneous slope ${L}_{t}^{\prime}$.
  • Theorem 6: The variance of the averaged slope $\hat{L}_{t}^{\prime}$ is different from the variance of the pointwise slope, ${L}_{t}^{\prime}$, and is given as
    • $\operatorname{var}\left(\hat{L}_{t}^{\prime}\right)=\frac{1}{N^{2}}\left(N \operatorname{var}\left(\left|\hat{y}_{t}\right|\right)+2 \sum_{i=1}^{N-1}(N-i) \operatorname{cov}\left(\left|\hat{y}_{t}\right|,\left|\hat{y}_{t-i}\right|\right)\right)$
  • Theorem 7: The expected value of the averaged entropy is the same as the pointwise value.
  • Theorem 8: The variance of the averaged entropy is different from the variance of the pointwise entropy and is given as
    • $\operatorname{var}\left(\overline{H}_{t}\right)=\frac{1}{N^{2}}\left(N \operatorname{var}\left(H_{t}\right)+2 \sum_{i=1}^{N-1}(N-i) \operatorname{cov}\left(H_{t}, H_{t-i}\right)\right)$
  • Theorem 9: The expected value of the differential entropy between the input and output does not depend on the standard deviation of the white noise process.

Theoretical Result for a First-Order System

  • Consider the classical, first-order process given as
    • $y_{t}=\frac{\beta z^{-1}}{1+\alpha z^{-1}} e_{t}$
      • where $e_t$ is a Gaussian signal
    • $\hat{y}_{t}=\frac{\beta z^{-1}-\beta z^{-2}}{1+\alpha z^{-1}} e_{t}$
    • $\begin{aligned} \mu &=0 \ \sigma_{\hat{y}}^{2} &=\frac{2 \beta^{2}}{1-\alpha} \sigma_{w}^{2} \end{aligned}$
  • The mean and variance
    • $E\left(\left|\hat{y}_{t}\right|\right)=\frac{2|\beta|}{\sqrt{(1-\alpha) \pi}} \sigma_{w}$
    • $\operatorname{var}\left(\left|\hat{y}_{t}\right|\right)=\frac{2 \beta^{2}}{1-\alpha}\left(\frac{\pi-2}{\pi}\right) \sigma_{w}^{2}$
  • from Theorem 2, the expected (either pointwise or averaged) slope of the length can be written as
    • $E\left(L_{t}^{\prime}\right)=\frac{2|\beta|}{\sqrt{(1-\alpha) \pi}} \sigma_{w}$
  • from Theorem 3, the expected (either pointwise or averaged) entropy can be written as
    • $E\left(H_{t}\right)=-\frac{1}{2} \log 2+\frac{1}{2} \log \left(\frac{2 \beta^{2}}{1-\alpha} \sigma_{w}^{2}\right)-\frac{1}{2} \gamma$
  • From Theorem 4, the variance of the pointwise entropy is
    • $\pi^{2} / 8$
  • The differential entropy
    • $\Delta H_{t}=\log \left(|\beta| \sqrt{\frac{1}{(1-\alpha)}}\right)$
  • It can be noted that the simulated and predicted values are in close agreement with each other.

Theoretical Result for a Second-Order System

  • Consider the standard, discrete-time, second-order system
    • $y_{t}=\frac{\beta_{1}+\beta_{2} z^{-1}}{\left(1+\alpha_{1} z^{-1}\right)\left(1+\alpha_{2} z^{-1}\right)} e_{t}$
    • $\hat{y}_{t}=\frac{\beta_{1} z^{2}+\left(\beta_{2}-\beta_{1}\right) z-\beta_{2}}{z^{2}+\left(\alpha_{1}+\alpha_{2}\right) z+\alpha_{1} \alpha_{2}} e_{t}$
  • The statistical properties of $\hat{y}_{t}$ are then given as
    • $\begin{aligned} \mu_{|\hat{y}|} &=0 \ \sigma_{|\hat{y}|}^{2} &=\frac{2\left(\alpha_{1} \alpha_{2} \beta_{1} \beta_{2}-\beta_{1} \alpha_{2} \beta_{2}-\beta_{1} \alpha_{1} \beta_{2}+\beta_{1}^{2}+\beta_{2}^{2}-\beta_{2} \beta_{1}\right)}{\left(\alpha_{2}-1\right)\left(1-\alpha_{2} \alpha_{1}\right)\left(\alpha_{1}-1\right)} \sigma_{w}^{2} \end{aligned}$
  • From Theorem 2, the expected entropy can be written as
    • $E\left(H_{t}\right)=\log \left(2 \sigma_{w} \sqrt{\frac{\left(\alpha_{1} \alpha_{2} \beta_{1} \beta_{2}-\beta_{1} \alpha_{2} \beta_{2}-\beta_{1} \alpha_{1} \beta_{2}+\beta_{1}^{2}+\beta_{2}^{2}-\beta_{2} \beta_{1}\right)}{\pi\left(\alpha_{2}-1\right)\left(1-\alpha_{2} \alpha_{1}\right)\left(\alpha_{1}-1\right)} )}\right)$
  • The entropy change can be written as
    • $\Delta H_{t}=\log \left(\sqrt{\frac{\left(\alpha_{1} \alpha_{2} \beta_{1} \beta_{2}-\beta_{1} \alpha_{2} \beta_{2}-\beta_{1} \alpha_{1} \beta_{2}+\beta_{1}^{2}+\beta_{2}^{2}-\beta_{2} \beta_{1}\right)}{\left(\alpha_{2}-1\right)\left(1-\alpha_{2} \alpha_{1}\right)\left(\alpha_{1}-1\right)}}\right)$
