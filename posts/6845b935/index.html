2.1 Scalars, Vectors, Matrices and Tensors

2.2 Multiplying Matrices and Vectors

  • matrix product
    • $\boldsymbol{C}=\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{B}$
    • $C_{i, j}=\sum_{k} A_{i, k} B_{k, j}$
  • element-wise product
    • $\boldsymbol{A} \odot \boldsymbol{B}$
  • dot product
    • $\boldsymbol{x} \cdot \boldsymbol{y} =\boldsymbol{x}^T \boldsymbol{y}$

2.3 Identity and Inverse Matrices 单位矩阵

  • identity matrix
    • $\boldsymbol{I}_{n} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$
    • $\forall \boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}, \boldsymbol{I}_{n} \boldsymbol{x}=\boldsymbol{x}$
  • matrix inverse
    • $\boldsymbol{A}^{-1}$
    • $\boldsymbol{A}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A}=\boldsymbol{I}_{n}$

2.4 Linear Dependence and Span 线性相关与生成子空间

  • The span of a set of vectors is the set of all points obtainable by linear combination of the original vectors.
  • Determining whether $\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{x}=\boldsymbol{b}$ has a solution thus amounts to testing whether b is in the span of the columns of $\boldsymbol{A}$.
    • This particular span is known as the column space 列空间, or the range 值域, of $\boldsymbol{A}$.
  • A set of vectors is linearly independent if no vector in the set is a linear combination of the other vectors.
  • Together, this means that the matrix must be square , that is, we require that m = n and that all the columns be linearly independent.
  • A square matrix with linearly dependent columns is known as singular.

2.5 Norms

  • $L^p$ norm
    • $|\boldsymbol{x}|_{p}=\left(\sum_{i}\left|x_{i}\right|^{p}\right)^{\frac{1}{p}}$
    • for $p \in \mathbb{R}, p \geq 1$
  • Norms, including the $L^p$ norm, are functions mapping vectors to non-negative values.
    • On an intuitive level, the norm of a vector $x$ measures the distance from the origin to the point $x$ .
  • More rigorously, a norm is any function $f$ that satisfies the following properties:
    • $f(\boldsymbol{x})=0 \Rightarrow \boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{0}$
    • $f(\boldsymbol{x}+\boldsymbol{y}) \leq f(\boldsymbol{x})+f(\boldsymbol{y})$, (the triangle inequality)
    • $\forall \alpha \in \mathbb{R}, f(\alpha \boldsymbol{x})=|\alpha| f(\boldsymbol{x})$
  • $L^2$ norm, Euclidean norm
    • $|\boldsymbol{x}|$
    • with the subscript 2 omitted
  • squared $L^2$ norm
    • $\boldsymbol{x}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}$
    • is more convenient to work with mathematically and computationally
      • each derivative of the squared L2 norm wrt. each element of x depends only on the corresponding element of x , while all the derivatives of the L2 norm depend on the entire vector.
    • but the squared L2 norm increases very slowly near the origin.
  • $L^1$ norm
    • $|x|_{1}=\sum_{i}\left|x_{i}\right|$
  • $L^\infty$ norm, max norm
    • $|\boldsymbol{x}|_{\infty}=\max _{i}\left|x_{i}\right|$
      • the absolute value of the element with the largest magnitude in the vector
  • Frobenius norm
    • measure the size of a matrix
    • $|\boldsymbol{A}|_{F}=\sqrt{\sum_{i, j} A_{i, j}^{2}}$
    • $|\boldsymbol{A}|_{F}=\sqrt{\operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{A}^{\top}\right)}$

2.6 Special Kinds of Matrices and Vectors

  • Diagonal matrices
  • symmetric matrix
  • unit vector
  • orthonormal
    • If the vectors not only are orthogonal but also have unit norm, we call them orthonormal.
  • orthogonal matrix
    • An orthogonal matrix is a square matrix whose rows are mutually orthonormal and whose columns are mutually orthonormal:
    • $\boldsymbol{A}^{\top} \boldsymbol{A}=\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{A}^{\top}=\boldsymbol{I}$
    • $\boldsymbol{A}^{-1}=\boldsymbol{A}^{\top}$
    • so orthogonal matrices are of interest because their inverse is very cheap to compute.

2.7 Eigendecomposition

  • $\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{v}=\lambda \boldsymbol{v}$
    • An eigenvector of a square matrix $\boldsymbol{A}$ is a nonzero vector $\boldsymbol{v}$ such that multiplication by alters only the scale of $\boldsymbol{v}$. The scalar $\lambda$ is known as the eigenvalue corresponding to this eigenvector. The scalar $\lambda$ is known as the eigenvalue corresponding to this eigenvector.
    • we usually look only for unit eigenvectors.
  • in this book, we usually need to decompose only a specific class of matrices that have a simple decomposition
    • $\boldsymbol{A}=\boldsymbol{Q} \boldsymbol{\Lambda} \boldsymbol{Q}^{\top}$

2.8 Singular Value Decomposition

  • The singular value decomposition (SVD) factorize a matrix into singular vectors and singular values .
  • $\boldsymbol{A}=\boldsymbol{U} \boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{V}^{\top}$
    • The matrices $\boldsymbol{U}$ and $\boldsymbol{V}$ are both defined to be orthogonal matrices. The matrix $\boldsymbol{D}$ is defined to be a diagonal matrix.
    • The left-singular vectors of $\boldsymbol{A}$ are the eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{A}^{\top}$ . The right-singular vectors of $\boldsymbol{A}$ are the eigenvectors of $\boldsymbol{A}^{\top} \boldsymbol{A}$. The nonzero singular values of $\boldsymbol{A}$ are the square roots of the eigenvalues of $\boldsymbol{A}^{\top} \boldsymbol{A}$. The same is true for $\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{A}^{\top}$.
  • we can use it to partially generalize matrix inversion to nonsquare matrices

2.9 The Moore-Penrose Pseudoinverse 伪逆

  • The pseudoinverse of $\boldsymbol{A}$ is defined as a matrix
    • $\boldsymbol{A}^{+}=\lim _{\alpha \searrow 0}\left(\boldsymbol{A}^{\top} \boldsymbol{A}+\alpha \boldsymbol{I}\right)^{-1} \boldsymbol{A}^{\top}$
  • Practical algorithms for computing
    • $\boldsymbol{A}^{+}=\boldsymbol{V} \boldsymbol{D}^{+} \boldsymbol{U}^{\top}$
    • the pseudoinverse $\boldsymbol{D}^{+}$ of a diagonal matrix $\boldsymbol{D}$ is obtained by taking the reciprocal of its nonzero elements then taking the transpose 非零元素求倒数再转置 of the resulting matrix.
  • the solution $\boldsymbol{x}=\boldsymbol{A}^{+} \boldsymbol{y}$ with minimal Euclidean norm $|x|_{2}$ among all possible solutions.

2.10 The Trace Operator

  • gives the sum of all the diagonal entries of a matrix:
    • $\operatorname{Tr}(\boldsymbol{A})=\sum_{i} \boldsymbol{A}_{i, i}$
  • Frobenius norm of a matrix:
    • $|A|_{F}=\sqrt{\operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{A}^{\top}\right)}$
  • the trace operator is invariant to the transpose operator.
    • $\operatorname{Tr}(\boldsymbol{A})=\operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{A}^{\top}\right)$
  • The trace of a square matrix
    • $\operatorname{Tr}(\boldsymbol{A B C})=\operatorname{Tr}(\boldsymbol{C A B})=\operatorname{Tr}(\boldsymbol{B C A})$
    • $\operatorname{Tr}\left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} \boldsymbol{F}^{(i)}\right)=\operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{F}^{(n)} \prod_{i=1}^{n-1} \boldsymbol{F}^{(i)}\right)$
  • for $\boldsymbol{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ and $\boldsymbol{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$
    • $\operatorname{Tr}(\boldsymbol{A} \boldsymbol{B})=\operatorname{Tr}(\boldsymbol{B} \boldsymbol{A})$

2.11 The Determinant

  • $\det(\boldsymbol{A})$
  • The determinant is equal to the product of all the eigenvalues of the matrix.

2.12 Example: Principal Components Analysis

  • Suppose we have a collection of pointsm ${\boldsymbol{x}^{(1)}, \ldots, \boldsymbol{x}^{(m)}}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$ and we want to apply lossy compression to these points.
  • For each point $\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ we will find a corresponding code vector $\boldsymbol{c}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{l}$.
    • $l$ is smaller than $n$
      • We will want to find some encoding function that produces the code for an input, $f(\boldsymbol{x}) = \boldsymbol{c}$ ,
      • and a decoding function that produces the reconstructed input given its code, $\boldsymbol{x} \approx g ( f( \boldsymbol{x})).$
  • PCA is defined by our choice of the decoding function.
    • we choose to use matrix multiplication to map the code back into $\mathbb{R}^{n}$.
    • Let $g(\boldsymbol{c})=\boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{c}$, where $\boldsymbol{D} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times l}$ is the matrix defining the decoding.
      • to simplify, PCA constrains the columns of $\boldsymbol{D}$ to be orthogonal to each other.
      • To give the problem a unique solution, we constrain all the columns of $\boldsymbol{D}$ to have unit norm.
    • the first thing we need to do is figure out how to generate the optimal code point $\boldsymbol{c}^{}$ for *each input point $\boldsymbol{x}$ .
      • minimize the distance between the input point $\boldsymbol{x}$ and its reconstruction, $g(\boldsymbol{c}^{*})$ .
        • $\boldsymbol{c}^{*}=\underset{\boldsymbol{c}}{\arg \min }|\boldsymbol{x}-g(\boldsymbol{c})|_{2}$
        • switch to the squared L2 norm instead of using the L2 norm
          • $\boldsymbol{c}^{*}=\underset{\boldsymbol{c}}{\arg \min }|\boldsymbol{x}-g(\boldsymbol{c})|_{2}^2$
        • The function being minimized simplifies to
          • $(\boldsymbol{x}-g(\boldsymbol{c}))^{\top}(\boldsymbol{x}-g(\boldsymbol{c}))$
          • $=\boldsymbol{x}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{x}^{\top} g(\boldsymbol{c})-g(\boldsymbol{c})^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}+g(\boldsymbol{c})^{\top} g(\boldsymbol{c})$
          • $=\boldsymbol{x}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}-2\boldsymbol{x}^{\top} g(\boldsymbol{c})+g(\boldsymbol{c})^{\top} g(\boldsymbol{c})$
          • omit the first term, since this term does not depend on $\boldsymbol{c}$
        • $\boldsymbol{c}^{*}=\underset{c}{\arg \min }-2 \boldsymbol{x}^{\top} g(\boldsymbol{c})+g(\boldsymbol{c})^{\top} g(\boldsymbol{c})$
        • substitute in the definition of $g(\boldsymbol{c})$
          • $\begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{c}^{*} &=\underset{c}{\arg \min }-2 \boldsymbol{x}^{\top} \boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{c}+\boldsymbol{c}^{\top} \boldsymbol{D}^{\top} \boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{c} \ &=\underset{c}{\arg \min }-2 \boldsymbol{x}^{\top} \boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{c}+\boldsymbol{c}^{\top} \boldsymbol{I}_{l} \boldsymbol{c} \&=\underset{c}{\arg \min }-2 \boldsymbol{x}^{\top} \boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{c}+\boldsymbol{c}^{\top} \boldsymbol{c} \end{aligned}$
          • $\nabla_{c}\left(-2 \boldsymbol{x}^{\top} \boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{c}+\boldsymbol{c}^{\top} \boldsymbol{c}\right)=\mathbf{0}$
            • $-2 \boldsymbol{D}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}+2 \boldsymbol{c}=\mathbf{0}$
            • $\boldsymbol{c}=\boldsymbol{D}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}$
      • This makes the algorithm efficient: we can optimally encode x using just a matrix-vector operation.
      • we can also define the PCA reconstruction operation:
        • $r(\boldsymbol{x})=g(f(\boldsymbol{x}))=\boldsymbol{D} \boldsymbol{D}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}$
    • Next, we need to choose the encoding matrix $\boldsymbol{D}$ .
      • Instead, we must minimize the Frobenius norm of the matrix of errors computed over all dimensions and all points:
      • $\boldsymbol{D}^{*}=\underset{\boldsymbol{D}}{\arg \min } \sqrt{\sum_{i, j}\left(x_{j}^{(i)}-r\left(\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}\right)_{j}\right)^{2}}$ subject to $\boldsymbol{D}^{\top} \boldsymbol{D}=\boldsymbol{I}_{l}$
        • start by considering the case where $l = 1$
        • $\boldsymbol{d}^{*}=\underset{\boldsymbol{d}}{\arg \min } \sum_{i}\left|\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}-\boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}\right|_{2}^{2}$ subject to $|\boldsymbol{d}|_{2}=1$
        • $\boldsymbol{d}^{*}=\underset{\boldsymbol{d}}{\arg \min } \sum_{i}\left|\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}-\boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}\boldsymbol{d} \right|_{2}^{2}$ subject to $|\boldsymbol{d}|_{2}=1$
        • $\boldsymbol{d}^{*}=\underset{\boldsymbol{d}}{\arg \min } \sum_{i}\left|\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)}-\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)\top} \boldsymbol{d}\boldsymbol{d} \right|_{2}^{2}$ subject to $|\boldsymbol{d}|_{2}=1$
        • $\boldsymbol{d}^{*}=\underset{\boldsymbol{d}}{\arg \min }\left|\boldsymbol{X}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right|_{F}^{2}$ subject to $\boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{d}=1$
          • $X \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}, \boldsymbol{X}_{i, :}=\boldsymbol{x}^{(i)^{\top}}$
      • Disregarding the constraint for the moment, we can simplify the Frobenius norm portion as follows:
        • $\underset{\boldsymbol{d}}{\arg \min }\left|\boldsymbol{X}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right|_{F}^{2}$
          • $=\underset{d}{\arg \min } \operatorname{Tr}\left(\left(\boldsymbol{X}-\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)^{\top}\left(\boldsymbol{X}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)\right)$
          • $=\underset{d}{\arg \min } \operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X}-\boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}-d \boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X}+d \boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)$
          • $=\underset{d}{\arg \min }-2 \operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)+\operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)$
          • $=\underset{\boldsymbol{d}}{\arg \min }-2 \operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)+\operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d} \boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)$ subject to $\boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{d}=1$
          • $=\underset{d}{\arg \min }-\operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d}\boldsymbol{d}^{\top}\right)$ subject to $\boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{d}=1$
          • $=\underset{\boldsymbol{d}}{\arg \max } \operatorname{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X}^{\top} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{d}\right)$ subject to $\boldsymbol{d}^{\top} \boldsymbol{d}=1$
      • $\lambda^n+(a{11}+a{22}+\cdots+a{nn})\lambda^{n-1}+a{11}a{22}\cdots{a{nn}}+\cdots=0$
